Overview

MusicLink is a music streaming app meant for enhancing the social experience through shared interests in music. Through MusicLink, users can see what their friends are listening, recommend music to their friends, and stay up to date with music recommendations catered to them. Over the course of 6 weeks, I designed MusicLink from with guidance and help from senior designers.

Problem

Music is an essential component of the human experience. People enjoy music and socialization, but user research revealed that people have a hard time expressing their interests in music with friends in a common place. Research showed that the following were problems experienced by users:

  1. Inability to see what their friends are listening to in the moment.
  2. Sharing playlists requires the use of multiple apps.
  3. Unable to see upcoming music events in their area without using multiple apps.

Solution

MusicLink provides an enhanced social experience created through music. Users can create friendships through shared interests in music with other users. Users are also able to discover new music through these interactions with other users in a fun and engaging way.

01 User Research

Survey

At this point of the process we knew we wanted the app to combine socializing and music, but the exact features were unknown. In an effort to create the MVP, we conducted user research. We began with a survey to examine the relationship between general public and their interest in music and socialization We asked about information they might be interested in sharing and seeing from regarding music streaming. We also asked questions about how they currently discover music and how they interested they were in possibly improving this process.

Key insights from survey

  1. Users are generally interested in socializing online through music.
  2. Current services make it difficult to engage in sharing music with others on a single platform.
  3. Not all users want to use Facebook for finding music events and would like a more centralized location.

Competitive Analysis

We began analyzing the current market in music streaming apps by looking at the three apps identified in user research: Youtube, Spotify, and Apple Music. All three music services allow users to stream music.

Spotify

02 Information Architecture

User Stories

Through user stories and personas, we were now able to create user stories to further narrow down the tasks that would be performed on the app. We prioritized the features based on the highest percentages found in the features section of the survey as well as the frustrations expressed in the round of user interviews.

  1. I want to easily see what music my friends are currently listening to.
  2. I want to easily see upcoming concerts in my area.
  3. I want to recommend music to friends within the same app.
  4. I want to easily create and share playlists with friends within the same app.

Wireframes

With rough sketches and the foundation for the architecture of the app, We began creating low-fidelity wireframes using Figma. In creating these wireframes, we focused on creating a seamless navigation experience throughout the app since this was something that was lacking in the apps analyzed in our competitive analysis.

Usability Testing: Round 1

The wireframes created allowed us to make a small prototype of key features we felt needed to be evaluated by users before moving forward. We created a prototype utilizing Maze which allowed us to test key features early on in the design process. Key emphasis was placed on completing tasks utilizing navigation because this was something we felt needed to be further researched and polished. Follow-up remote testing via Zoom was also conducted.

When asked to open the song menu and recommend a song to a friend on the app, users were confused on where to click. Users said they were confused by the “add to playlist” icon and thought that it represented a “more” icon. This may be due to the fact that the icon has a plus sign and that it is at the end, suggesting more options.

First set of changes

  1. The menu option was removed from this location.
  2. The menu option was moved to the location where the ‘add to playlist’ icon was for better visibility.
  3. The add to playlist option was embedded into the menu that follows after users click on the menu icon.

Second set of changes

  1. Through further research on the navigation of the app, we realized playlists would be the only type of content to be created within the app. The option to create a new playlist was therefore moved to a more appropriate place.
  2. The tab bar was simplified and included everything that was accessible in the app.

03 Visual Design

Branding

To give life to the wireframes with visual design, the first step was to begin branding for the app. We started with a mind mapping exercise to get a feel for what the app would potentially be. We focused on words such as music, friendship and connected. The mind mapping activity ultimately led to us to choose the name “MusicLink” for the app.

Logo & Color Palette

The logo sketches and digital renditions utilized concepts explored in the mind mapping exercise. The final logo incorporates the shape of the letter “M” with the ends representing music notes.

The first rendition of the logo utilized a color palette that would later be changed. Through advice from other designers, it was suggested that the main color of the app be changed due to it being similar to the color used in Apple Music.

Establishing a Design System

In an effort to keep designs consistent across different screens, we established a small design system. Using Figma's color styles, text styles, and assets features made it easier to establish a cohesive system and simplified the overall design process. This was especially valuable when having to make overall edits to designs based on user testing.

04 High Fidelity Mockups & Testing

Usability Testing: Round 2

With visuals and a small design system incorporated into the wireframes that were iterated upon after user testing, it was time to conduct further usability testing. Remote testing and another Maze test were completed in order to test out the usability of the app.

Task 1: Sharing a playlist from user profile

Maze testing revealed a 83% misclick rate, meaning users were very confused as to where to click to open the menu that would enable them to share a playlist. Users that asked to complete this task via the Zoom testing exhibited the same confusion on this screen.

  1. Maze testing revealed that users thought the “edit profile” button meant to share or make a post, so this was changed to a button that distinctly reads “edit profile”.
  2. Users were confused by the prototype where it referred to “Max” on the user profile, so the wording was changed to “Your playlists” for an experience centered around the user.
  3. The playlist cards were edited to include the last time they were played and the icon to open the menu, where users would select to share the playlist, was moved and made into a clearer call to action.
  4. With further discussion with other designers, we came to the agreement that this screen felt stark and distinct from the rest of the app. Changes in the overall UI were implemented for an overall cleaner look that followed the design system previously established.

Task 2: Creating a playlist

When completing this task, some users commented on this flow being unnecessarily long. To shorten and simplify the flow a user would take to create a new playlist, the CTA for creating a new playlist was moved up to the first screen in the flow. In addition to simplifying this flow, it made sense to move this CTA here because this screen felt bare before and simply served as a navigational screen.

Task 3: Visiting friend's profile via the home feed

User testing revealed that users found the card system showing what friends were listening to on the home feed to be confusing when trying to click on certain things. Particularly, users found it confusing if they wanted to click on the fried's profile rather than the song they are listening to, and vice versa.

Initial design

Redesign

  1. Friend information and music information were ungrouped.
  2. More space alotted to the song the friend is listening because this is the most pertinent information for this section.

Task 4: Finding local music events to attend

Testing revealed that users found it confusing to begin browsing local music events. Maze testing showed that some users clicked on the "discover" icon on the tab bar rather than the "local" tab on the segmented navigation of the homepage.

  1. The "local" tab was renamed to "events".
  2. A search function was added to this screen because users would ideally want to look for events in other locations, especially if they are travelling.

Reflection

What worked?

The overall structure of the app and design worked well in the beginning stages of the design. Due to the simplicity of the design, users were able to easily locate call to actions and locations in which key pages were held. User testing was also essential for reaching a better design and ultimately led to what features would be included and helped me simplify the user flows and overall user experience.

What didn't work?

The overall onboarding experience felt choppy and abrupt at times. The experience should have been more interactive, but it was difficult to personalize the experience without having the user sign up first.

If I had more time

If given more time, I would have definitely focused more on the onboarding experience. I would have also spent more time on the design system. Particularly, I had a hard time finding icons for the app in a short amount of time. As a result, some icon choices do not feel cohesive.
