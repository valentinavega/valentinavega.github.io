MARTA On The Go

A redesign of Atlanta's MARTA On The Go iOS app.

View Prototype

Overview

Atlanta’s public transit system, MARTA, is widely used within the city. The primary ridership is made up of existing residents of Atlanta and new residents. Although there are many distinct faces that make up MARTA’s ridership, they can all agree on one thing: MARTA’s mobile app needs improvement. Over the course of 4 weeks, I redesigned MARTA's app for an overall enhanced experience.

Problem

MARTA’s iOS app presents a bad case of User Experience. The problems affect a large range of users and directly impacts their experience with MARTA not just through the app, but in the real world as well. Particularly, I identified the following as key problem points in the MARTA app:

  1. Confusing Information Architecture - information is not presented in a hierarchy that serves user needs.
  2. Navigational issues - too many steps required to complete a user flow.
  3. Outdated UI - this only enhances the previous issues and causes more confusion for users (double use of icons, confusing color choices, etc.)

Solution

Through user research and thorough examination of the existing app, as well as the MARTA system overall, I redesigned an enhanced MARTA app experience that focuses on user needs. Instead of throwing transit information at users, the redesign allows for users to:

Locate that Bus

Easily navigate the app to find where the bus you're planning to take is via GPS without losing time or potentially missing the bus.

Plan a Trip

Get to know your way around Atlanta and plan a trip within the MARTA app without having to use different apps all at the same time.

Catch the right train

Easily find when the next train is coming through a color coded system.

Preliminary Research

Survey

I conducted a short survey to better understand the needs of users that currently use MARTA and its mobile app. The following findings served as a basis for determining current frustrations with the app and how a redesign would improve them.

Personas

Since I am currently not living in Atlanta, I was only able to conduct 3 remote interviews. I created the following personas based on the survey findings and interviews:

Goals

• Easily see where she is on a map because she is new to Atlanta.
• Easily find nearby bus stops so that she doesn’t spend too much time figuring out what bus to take before class.

“I spent around 30 minutes just trying to figure out the correct buses to take once and ended up taking uber. This was more expensive of course.”

Frustrations

• Cannot easily locate what buses she should take from her apartment to college.
• Cannot plan a trip easily within the app.

Goals

• Easily access his saved buses and train lines under ‘favorites’.
• An easy to read and understand app with intuitive navigation.

“It would be nice to have something that is easy to read and navigate – something that doesn’t feel like I’m going down a rabbithole when I just want to find the bus schedule.”

Frustrations

• Loses track of where he is on the app.
• Cannot easily locate his saved routes, which causes him to spend too much time figuring out when the next bus or train will arrive.

Additional Findings

Due to my limited access to users of the app, I resorted to analyzing the app reviews to get an even better sense of current user frustrations and goals. I found that frustrations commonly revolved around app navigation and overall usability of the real-time bus feature.

Validated Assumptions:

  1. Current users are both new and existing Atlanta residents
  2. Users want to easily access their most utilized features.
  3. Users would like to easily access Breeze Card information.

New Findings:

  1. Streetcar feature is the least utilized within the app.
  2. Older population (65 and older) currently utilize the app as well.
  3. Users would like the ability to plan a trip directly within the app.

Information Architecture

User Stories

With the information I attained through research, I was able to narrow down the key tasks users needed to complete in the app and prioritized them.

User Flows

I then moved onto creating user flows to map out exactly how a user would navigate the app. When creating these flows, emphasis was placed upon reducing the amount of taps required to locate transit information because users I interviewed commented on how this often affected their ability to catch a train or bus on time.

“As a new user, I want check the real time bus map for my route.”

“As a new user, I want to quickly check the upcoming train times.”

Usability Testing and Iterations: Part 1

Remote testing was conducted using maze.design on both existing MARTA On The Go users (n = 20) and new users. Overall, the design of the app was intuitive to users. However, there were still issues relating to the information architecture of the app – particularly in the navigation. When asked to find buses near them, users took longer to find this feature and clicked on incorrect parts of the screens.

Iteration 1

Iteration 2

  1. I reorganized the navigation of the app and made the nearby feature the first menu item. This solution also ensures a more user-centered app experience. This also supports the finding from the survey that users primarily open the app when they are looking for a bus around them to see when it is arriving.
  2. The copy was also changed from “Routes” to Transit because some users stated that “Routes” made them think this section only housed bus information.

Visual Design

For the visual design of this app, I utilized MARTA’s existing branding and colors throughout the app. One of the pain points in the current app is the outdated UI, so in my redesign, I ensured the design utilized a clear system. Color and visual hierarchy were used to highlight the most important information to the user.

Usability Testing and Iterations: Part 2

To validate my UI design decisions, I conducted more remote usability testing. My findings from this session of testing called for slight changes in the UI to enhance overall usability – particularly in the way the information was presented.

Iteration 1

Iteration 2

  1. One user noted that they associated orange with urgency, so I changed the color used for bus information to a cool-toned color.
  2. Two users noted that some of the copy on here was hard to read, so I darkened the colors and adjusted the visual hierarchy.
  3. Colors and icons to highlight urgency were added to the information about the bus being nearby and how long of a walk it would be for the user.

Reflection

Overall, this project was a good way to improve both the information architecture of an app and interface. I was able to put my UX and UI skills to test on a usability problem I encountered for years before I transitioned into UX. It was satisfying to design solutions for these usability issues that bugged me for years but this created another challenge I had to overcome: Seeing beyond my own user bias. For example, I never thought the planning a trip feature would be a useful addition to the app, but an overwhelming majority of my participants in my research voiced their need for this feature and it made a lot of sense when I considered the some of the demographics – international students relying on Marta and learning their way around town at the same time.

If I had more time

There are a lot of things to be incorporated in the future to go beyond the MVP. I would like to be able to conduct in-person research with potential users of the redesigned app. Being able to see how users would navigate the app in different environments such as a Marta bus stop or station would give me even more insight on how to possibly make this even better.
